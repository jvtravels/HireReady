### Persona 1: Marcus Chen — "The Displaced Executive"

**Demographics**

- Age: 48
- Location: Chicago, IL
- Title: Former VP of Operations (Fortune 500 manufacturing company)
- Household income: $220K (prior), currently on severance
- Education: MBA, Northwestern Kellogg

**Bio**

Marcus spent 16 years climbing the ladder at a single company. When a restructuring eliminated his division, he found himself job-hunting for the first time in over a decade. He's confident in his abilities but deeply rusty on interviewing — the last time he sat across from a hiring panel, smartphones barely existed. He's embarrassed to admit he needs practice and won't ask colleagues for help. He wants to rebuild his confidence privately before stepping into real interviews.

**Goals**

- Practice realistic executive-level interviews without anyone knowing
- Get honest, data-driven feedback on blind spots he can't self-assess (filler words, rambling, weak storytelling)
- Prepare for specific roles he's targeting — not generic "tell me about yourself" drills
- Feel interview-ready within 2–3 weeks

**Frustrations**

- Existing prep tools feel juvenile — quiz-style flashcards designed for college grads, not VPs
- Hiring a human coach costs $200–400/session and requires scheduling weeks out
- YouTube videos give theory but no personalized practice or feedback
- He doesn't want to practice with friends or family — it feels unprofessional and the feedback is unreliable
- Most platforms require credit cards upfront for trials, which feels like a trap

**Behaviors**

- Discovers tools via LinkedIn posts, industry newsletters, and Google searches at midnight
- Will try one free session to evaluate quality before paying anything
- Makes purchase decisions fast if the product delivers — money is not the primary barrier, quality is
- Uses a MacBook Pro exclusively; occasionally checks things on his iPhone but prefers desktop for serious work
- Privacy-conscious: reads terms of service, wants to know where his data goes, expects easy account deletion

**Relationship with Level Up**

- Enters via B2C landing page, drawn by "Start Free Mock Interview" with no credit card required
- Completes 1 free mock targeting a COO role at a mid-size company
- Impressed by the role-specific feedback and score breakdown — upgrades to Weekly ($29) immediately
- Uses it intensively for 2 weeks (8–10 sessions), focusing on different interview scenarios
- Upgrades to Pro ($199/quarter) for deeper analytics and to track improvement over time
- Stops using after landing a new role; exports his data as a PDF keepsake; deletes account

**Key Quotes**

> "I don't need someone to teach me how to interview. I need a sparring partner who pushes back and tells me where I'm weak."

> "If this looks like it was designed for interns, I'm closing the tab in 3 seconds."

**Design Implications**

- The luxury aesthetic is critical for Marcus — he equates design quality with product quality
- Free trial with no credit card is the only way he'll try it
- Executive-level question generation is essential; generic behavioral questions won't retain him
- Privacy controls (export, delete) must be visible and trustworthy
- Desktop-first experience matters most, but mobile should work for review/feedback

### Persona 2: Priya Sharma — "The Strategic Climber"

**Demographics**

- Age: 34
- Location: Austin, TX
- Title: Senior Product Manager at a Series C startup
- Household income: $165K
- Education: BS Computer Science, MS Product Management

**Bio**

Priya isn't job-hunting — she's preparing. She's been told she's on the shortlist for a Director of Product role at her company, and the promotion process includes a formal interview panel with the C-suite. She wants to sharpen her executive presence and practice articulating strategic decisions under pressure. She's also quietly interviewing at two other companies as leverage. She's ambitious, data-driven, and treats career advancement like a product launch — with milestones, metrics, and iteration.

**Goals**

- Practice articulating product strategy and leadership philosophy in interview format
- Get quantified feedback she can track over time — she wants to see improvement curves
- Prepare for both internal promotion interviews and external FAANG-level director interviews
- Build a library of polished answers to common strategic questions

**Frustrations**

- Mock interview platforms don't understand the difference between PM interviews and generic behavioral interviews
- She can't practice with her current team without revealing she's exploring external opportunities
- Free tools are low-quality; paid human coaching is good but too slow for her timeline
- She wants to practice at 10pm after her kids are in bed — no scheduling, no waiting

**Behaviors**

- Researches tools methodically — reads reviews, compares features, checks for product-specific interview support
- Will pay immediately if the free trial demonstrates personalization and quality
- Uses the platform in short, focused bursts (2–3 sessions per week over 6 weeks)
- Tracks her scores in a personal spreadsheet alongside the platform's analytics
- Switches between MacBook and iPad; expects seamless responsive experience
- Shares the platform with 2–3 trusted peers via DM if she likes it — organic referral source

**Relationship with Level Up**

- Finds Level Up via a Product Management Slack community recommendation
- Signs up, specifies "Director of Product" as target role, uploads resume
- Free mock generates PM-specific questions about roadmap prioritization, stakeholder management, technical tradeoffs
- Upgrades to Pro ($199/quarter) on Day 1 — she wants the analytics and challenge features immediately
- Uses practice challenges ("Try a C-level mock") to simulate board-level conversations
- After 6 weeks, lands the Director role internally; keeps her account active for occasional sharpening

**Key Quotes**

> "I don't just want to practice — I want to measure my improvement week over week."

> "The questions need to be specific to my role and level. If I hear 'What's your greatest weakness?' I'm out."

**Design Implications**

- Pro tier analytics (improvement trends, session comparison) are high value for this persona
- Role-specific question generation is non-negotiable — PM, engineering, executive, etc.
- Practice challenges keep her engaged beyond initial prep
- Mobile/tablet experience matters — she practices on her iPad after hours
- Export features (PDF/CSV) support her personal tracking habit

### Persona 3: Dana Whitfield — "The Solo Coach"

**Demographics**

- Age: 41
- Location: Denver, CO
- Title: Founder, Whitfield Career Partners (solo practice)
- Revenue: $180K/year
- Education: MS Organizational Psychology, ICF-certified coach

**Bio**

Dana left corporate HR five years ago to start her own career coaching practice. She works with 8–12 clients at a time, mostly senior managers and directors navigating transitions. Her coaching is deeply personal and high-touch, but she struggles with the interview prep portion — she can only do so many mock interviews herself before it cuts into strategy sessions. She needs a tool that lets her clients practice independently while she focuses on the higher-value coaching work (resume strategy, networking, salary negotiation).

**Goals**

- Give clients unlimited, high-quality interview practice without consuming her 1:1 time
- Track client progress with objective data to supplement her subjective assessments
- Demonstrate measurable ROI to justify her $5K–$8K coaching packages
- Maintain her personal brand — tools she recommends reflect on her professionalism

**Frustrations**

- Free interview prep tools are embarrassingly low quality — she can't recommend them to $8K clients
- She manually tracks client interview readiness in spreadsheets and Notion
- When clients need practice, she either does it herself (burning billable hours) or tells them to "practice with a friend" (unhelpful)
- Most B2B SaaS tools are designed for 50+ seat enterprises — too complex for a solo practice with 10 clients
- She doesn't want to commit to an annual contract without seeing if clients actually use the tool

**Behaviors**

- Discovers tools through coaching communities, ICF conferences, and peer referrals
- Will start a free pilot immediately if no credit card is required
- Evaluates based on client experience quality — she'll test it herself first, then onboard 1–2 clients during trial
- Needs the admin dashboard to be simple and fast — she spends 5 minutes/day max on platform admin
- Makes purchase decisions based on client feedback and usage data during the pilot
- Price-sensitive on per-seat costs since she's a solo operator

**Relationship with Level Up**

- Finds Level Up via the B2B landing page (`/for-teams`) linked from a coaching community post
- Starts 1-month free pilot, adds 2 clients immediately
- Monitors client usage daily during the first week, then weekly
- Clients report the AI feedback is "surprisingly good" — better than peer practice
- At trial end, receives summary report showing client engagement and score improvements
- Upgrades to annual license (per-seat) for her active client roster
- Gradually onboards all new clients onto the platform as part of her standard coaching package

**Key Quotes**

> "My clients are paying me $8,000. If I send them to a tool that looks cheap, it undermines everything."

> "I need to see their progress without asking them to send me screenshots. Just give me a dashboard."

**Design Implications**

- B2B landing page must speak to solo coaches, not just enterprise firms
- Pilot onboarding must be dead simple — she'll abandon if setup takes more than 15 minutes
- Admin dashboard should surface the 3–4 metrics she cares about without overwhelming detail
- Per-client progress view is critical — she references it in coaching sessions
- The platform quality (design, AI feedback) reflects on her personal brand

### Persona 4: James Okafor — "The Firm Operator"

**Demographics**

- Age: 52
- Location: Atlanta, GA
- Title: Managing Director, Okafor Transition Group (outplacement firm)
- Company size: 14 employees, serves 200+ candidates/year
- Education: MBA, Emory; SHRM-SCP certified

**Bio**

James runs a mid-size outplacement firm contracted by corporations to support laid-off employees through career transitions. His firm handles resume writing, networking coaching, and interview preparation. Interview prep is the most labor-intensive service — his team of 6 coaches each conducts 3–5 mock interviews per day, and they're at capacity. He needs a tool that scales interview practice without hiring more coaches, while providing the data his corporate clients demand in quarterly reviews.

**Goals**

- Scale interview prep capacity without proportionally scaling headcount
- Provide corporate clients with engagement metrics and outcome data
- Standardize interview prep quality across his coaching team
- Reduce time-to-placement for candidates (currently averaging 4.2 months)

**Frustrations**

- His coaches spend 40% of their time on mock interviews that could be AI-augmented
- Corporate clients demand quarterly reports showing engagement and outcomes — his team assembles these manually
- He's evaluated tools before, but they either lack quality or don't support multi-client management
- Enterprise SaaS tools require 6-month procurement cycles; he needs something he can pilot immediately
- His IT team is one person — the tool must be low-maintenance

**Behaviors**

- Evaluates tools through a business lens: ROI, scalability, reporting capability
- Will pilot with a small cohort before committing budget — needs to see usage data and candidate feedback
- Decision-making involves his operations manager and lead coach
- Expects vendor support for onboarding and quarterly reviews
- Pays via invoice, not credit card — annual contracts are standard
- Will push for volume discounts on per-seat pricing

**Relationship with Level Up**

- Operations manager finds Level Up, shares with James
- James reviews B2B landing page, watches demo video, starts free pilot
- Assigns 2 candidates from a current corporate engagement to trial
- Lead coach monitors candidates' usage and provides feedback on AI quality
- After 3 weeks, candidates report positive experience; James sees usage data in admin dashboard
- Negotiates annual license with volume pricing for 50+ seats
- Rolls out to all active candidates; coaches use the platform data to supplement their 1:1 sessions
- Uses export features to generate quarterly reports for corporate clients

**Key Quotes**

> "I don't need another tool my coaches have to babysit. I need something candidates use independently and coaches reference in sessions."

> "Show me the data. If I can't prove ROI to my corporate clients, this doesn't matter."

**Design Implications**

- Admin reports must support corporate reporting use cases (exportable, professional)
- Bulk invite (CSV upload) is essential for 50+ candidate onboarding
- Client table needs robust sorting/filtering for managing large cohorts
- Per-seat pricing must accommodate volume — this persona will negotiate
- Priority support expectation — he's paying for a business-critical tool
- Invoice/PO payment option may be needed post-MVP

### Persona 5: Aaliyah Torres — "The Coached Client"

**Demographics**

- Age: 39
- Location: Miami, FL
- Title: Former Regional Sales Director (recently laid off via corporate restructuring)
- Education: BA Communications, various sales certifications

**Bio**

Aaliyah was laid off 6 weeks ago when her company consolidated regional offices. Her employer contracted Okafor Transition Group (James's firm) to provide outplacement services. Her coach Dana (or one of James's team members) has been working with her on resume optimization and networking strategy. Now it's time for interview prep, and her coach has sent her an email invite to Level Up Interviews. Aaliyah didn't choose this tool — it was assigned to her. She's skeptical but willing to try if it helps her land faster.

**Goals**

- Get interview-ready as quickly as possible — she has 3 final-round interviews in 2 weeks
- Practice answering sales leadership questions with realistic pressure
- Understand what she's doing well and where she's falling short — specific, actionable feedback
- Feel confident, not judged — she's already dealing with the emotional weight of a layoff

**Frustrations**

- She didn't choose this tool and doesn't want to spend time learning yet another platform
- She's nervous about being recorded and having an AI assess her performance
- Generic feedback ("be more specific") isn't helpful — she needs examples and concrete improvement steps
- She's juggling job applications, networking events, and coaching sessions — her time is fragmented
- She's doing this on her phone between appointments as often as on her laptop

**Behaviors**

- Receives an email invite from her coach with a link to create her account
- First interaction is skeptical — she'll decide within the first 5 minutes if this is worth her time
- If the first interview feels realistic and the feedback is specific, she'll use it repeatedly
- Practices in 15–20 minute sessions between other activities
- Shares results with her coach in their next session — the coach references her Level Up scores
- Uses mobile as often as desktop — needs full functionality at 375px

**Relationship with Level Up**

- Receives invite email from coach → clicks link → creates account (email confirmation required)
- Enters resume and target role ("VP of Sales, SaaS company")
- Completes first mock interview on her phone during a lunch break
- Feedback highlights she's underselling her team management experience — specific, useful
- Practices 4 more times over the next week, focusing on the areas flagged
- Coach reviews her progress in admin dashboard; they discuss scores in their next session
- Lands a VP of Sales role; coach exports her session data for the firm's success metrics

**Key Quotes**

> "I don't have time to figure out how this works. If it's not obvious in 30 seconds, I'm closing it."

> "The AI told me I was underselling my team's revenue growth by 40% — my coach said the same thing last week. Okay, now I trust it."

**Design Implications**

- Invite acceptance flow must be frictionless — 3 clicks to first interview
- Mobile experience is primary for this persona, not secondary
- Emotional design matters: encouraging language, progress celebration, non-judgmental tone in feedback
- AI feedback must be specific and actionable, not generic
- She didn't choose this tool — onboarding must prove value immediately or she'll disengage
- Audio recording consent must be clear and non-threatening

## Design Principles Derived from Personas

1. **Quality signals trust.** Marcus and Dana equate design quality with product quality. The luxury first-touch isn't vanity — it's a conversion lever for professionals who dismiss tools that look cheap.

2. **Prove value in the first session.** Aaliyah didn't choose this tool. Priya is evaluating alternatives. Marcus will close the tab in 3 seconds. The free trial must deliver undeniably specific, useful feedback on the very first mock interview.

3. **Respect time fragmentation.** Aaliyah practices between appointments on her phone. Priya practices at 10pm. Nobody has a dedicated hour for this — sessions should feel complete in 15–20 minutes and the UI should load fast on any device.

4. **Data is currency.** Priya tracks improvement over time. James reports to corporate clients. Dana references scores in coaching sessions. The scoring and analytics aren't nice-to-haves — they're the reason these personas pay.

5. **Privacy isn't a checkbox.** Marcus reads the terms. Dana's clients are going through vulnerable career moments. Data export, deletion, and retention controls must feel trustworthy and be genuinely functional — not buried compliance theater.
